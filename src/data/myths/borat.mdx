## The answer in 20 seconds

Borat is a fictional character, not a factual representative of Kazakhstan.<sup><a href="#claim-C1">C1</a></sup> Academic work and reporting describe the on-screen “Kazakhstan” as a constructed setting assembled from stereotypes and imagery that do not meaningfully correspond to the real country.<sup><a href="#claim-C2">C2</a></sup>

> A Borat reference is a reference to a British satirical character. It is not evidence about Kazakh people, language, history or everyday life.

## What is fictional?

### The person

Borat Sagdiyev is written and performed by Sacha Baron Cohen. Reference and catalogue descriptions identify him as a fictional foreign correspondent inside a mockumentary format.<sup><a href="#claim-C1">C1</a></sup>

### The country around him

Scholars have argued that the films create a substitute “Kazakhstan” from generalized eastern-European and Balkan imagery. That invented setting may become more familiar to an international viewer than the real country whose name it uses.<sup><a href="#claim-C2">C2</a></sup>

### The village

The village footage shown as Borat's home was filmed in Glod, Romania—not Kazakhstan. Contemporary reporting also documented complaints from Romanian residents about how they were represented and what they believed they were participating in.<sup><a href="#claim-C3">C3</a></sup>

## What was the satire aimed at?

The films are commonly framed as satire built around Borat's encounters with people in the United States. The character's extreme prejudice and ignorance create situations intended to expose or provoke prejudice in others.<sup><a href="#claim-C4">C4</a></sup>

That intended target does not erase every side effect. The films still connect a real country's name to an invented society portrayed as uniquely backward, violent and bigoted. It is possible to understand the satire's main target while also understanding why some Kazakhstanis object to the framing.

## Why do some Kazakhstanis object?

The objection is usually not that viewers literally believe every scene. It is that Kazakhstan was unfamiliar to many international audiences, so the fictional version could become their first or only reference point. Kazakhstan's government responded aggressively around the first film, including public criticism; the film did not receive normal theatrical distribution in the country.<sup><a href="#claim-C5">C5</a></sup>

Official reaction should not be confused with the opinion of every citizen. Government image management and personal experiences of stereotyping are different questions.

## Do Kazakhstanis all agree?

No. Reporting in 2020 recorded several positions: people who regarded the films as insulting or racist; people who saw the main joke as being about Americans; people who considered public outrage excessive; and people who preferred to convert the attention into tourism publicity.<sup><a href="#claim-C7">C7</a></sup>

A selection of interviews can document a range of views. It cannot prove what an entire country thinks.

## Why did tourism use “Very nice!” later?

In 2020, Kazakhstan's tourism organization published an official campaign using the catchphrase “Very nice!” and government reporting listed the campaign among its promotional work.<sup><a href="#claim-C6">C6</a></sup> That decision shows a strategic change in official communication. It does not prove that earlier objections disappeared or that all Kazakhstanis approved.

## What can safely be said about the real country?

Kazakhstan is a sovereign state that became independent in 1991. Kazakh is the state language, and Russian has an officially defined public role.<sup><a href="#claim-C8">C8</a></sup> Those basic facts already show why the film should not be treated as cultural documentation: the invented language, institutions and customs on screen are not a compressed version of the real country.

## A respectful way to use the reference

- Treat Borat as a fictional British comedy character.
- Do not describe his speech, customs or biography as Kazakh culture.
- Do not claim that every Kazakhstani is offended—or that nobody is.
- When someone says the reference has repeatedly been used to mock them, do not dismiss that experience solely because the intended target of the satire was different.
- For factual claims about Kazakhstan, use sources about Kazakhstan rather than details from the film.
