## The answer in 20 seconds

Kazakhstan is a Muslim-majority country with a secular constitution. These two statements are compatible. The state does not define itself as an Islamic republic, while Islam remains an important part of family life, history, culture and public identity. Other religious communities are also present.<sup><a href="#claim-C1">C1</a></sup>

## Secular does not mean anti-religious

In constitutional language, secularism means that the state is not governed by an official religion and that public authority is separated from religious institutions. It does not require citizens to be non-believers or prevent religious practice. A secular state can contain religious families, mosques, churches, synagogues and people who identify with no religion.<sup><a href="#claim-C1">C1</a></sup>

## Islam and Kazakh identity

Islam has deep historical roots among Kazakh communities, but identity is not a single switch. People relate to religion through belief, family custom, holidays, morality, community and personal practice in different ways. Some are observant, some are culturally Muslim, some combine local custom with religious practice, and some are secular or unaffiliated.

## A plural society

Kazakhstan also has Christian communities and smaller Jewish, Buddhist, Baháʼí and other religious groups. The country's multi-ethnic history means religion, language and ethnicity do not map perfectly onto one another. A Russian-speaking citizen may be Muslim; a Kazakh citizen may be non-observant; a family may contain several religious backgrounds. These combinations are ordinary features of a diverse society.<sup><a href="#claim-C2">C2</a></sup>

## Religion in public culture

Religious identity can appear through holidays, family ceremonies, food practices, clothing, charity and ideas about hospitality without becoming a demand for a religious government. Nauryz, for example, has pre-Islamic, Islamic and modern civic layers in the way people describe and celebrate it. A public cultural symbol does not automatically show that the state has adopted a religious law.

## Why the two stereotypes coexist

Outside observers sometimes look at a mosque, a Muslim name or a religious holiday and infer an Islamic state. Others look at Soviet history, urban life or secular law and infer that religion is irrelevant. Both readings mistake one visible layer for the whole society. The better question is how belief, law, institutions and personal practice interact in different settings. That approach is more accurate than importing assumptions from another country.

## The legal qualification

Freedom of belief exists within a regulated legal environment. International monitors have raised concerns about registration requirements, missionary activity and restrictions affecting some religious groups. That means “secular” should not be used as a synonym for “religious freedom is unlimited.” Constitutional principle and lived enforcement must be examined separately.<sup><a href="#claim-C3">C3</a></sup>

Religious life and secular law coexist in contemporary Kazakhstan.

## Better wording

Say **"Kazakhstan is a secular state with a Muslim-majority population and religious diversity"**, not **"Kazakhstan is an Islamic state"** or **"religion has no role there."**
