> Describing Kazakhstan as ethnically homogeneous erases the lived reality of millions of its residents.

## Who lives in Kazakhstan?

### The Kazakh majority

Ethnic Kazakhs are the dominant group and the group whose name the country carries. Kazakh is the state language, and official policy since independence has encouraged its use in public institutions. The Kazakh community itself is not monolithic: it includes different tribal confederations (zhuz), urban and rural populations, and diaspora returnees (oralmans) who came primarily from China, Mongolia, Russia and Uzbekistan after 1991.

### The Russian community

Russians make up the second-largest group — roughly 15 to 17 percent — and are concentrated in the north and in major cities such as Almaty and Astana.<sup><a href="#claim-C2">C2</a></sup> Russian remains in widespread daily use and has an officially defined role in public institutions.

### Other communities

Uzbeks, Ukrainians, Uyghurs, Germans, Tatars, Koreans, Azerbaijanis, Belarusians, Dungans, Poles and dozens of additional communities each have a presence in the country.<sup><a href="#claim-C1">C1</a></sup> Many maintain their own cultural organizations, media and schools.

## Why is Kazakhstan so diverse?

The answer lies in layers of history.

### Pre-Soviet history

Kazakh nomadic confederations interacted and intermixed with neighbouring Turkic, Mongol and Iranian peoples over centuries. The steppe economy depended on exchange, and the territory's populations were never sealed off.

### Soviet-era forced resettlements

Starting in the 1930s and accelerating during the Second World War, Soviet authorities forcibly deported entire peoples to Kazakhstan. Koreans from the Soviet Far East were moved in 1937. Germans, Chechens, Ingush, Karachays, Balkars, Poles, Crimean Tatars and others followed across the 1940s.<sup><a href="#claim-C3">C3</a></sup> Most of these communities rebuilt lives in Kazakhstan and their descendants remain.

### Industrial migration

Soviet industrialisation brought large numbers of Slavic and other workers to Kazakhstan's mines, factories and construction sites, particularly during the Virgin Lands campaign of the 1950s. This migration changed the demographic balance of entire regions.

### Post-independence patterns

After 1991, some Russian and other Slavic residents emigrated, while Kazakh diaspora members returned from neighbouring countries. Both flows continued over subsequent decades, shifting the proportions without eliminating minority communities.

## Does diversity create tension?

The question has no single answer.

Kazakhstan has avoided the open ethnic conflicts that appeared elsewhere in the former Soviet space. The government credits the Assembly of the People of Kazakhstan and official multi-ethnicity policy.<sup><a href="#claim-C4">C4</a></sup> Independent observers and international organizations have noted that language policy — specifically the transition from Russian to Kazakh in public institutions — requires careful management to avoid disadvantaging Russian-speaking communities.<sup><a href="#claim-C5">C5</a></sup>

The existence of a policy framework does not mean all tensions are resolved. It means the country officially recognizes diversity as something that must be managed, not ignored.

## What this means in practice

Encountering a Kazakhstani person does not tell you which ethnic community they belong to, which language they grew up speaking or how they identify. Making that assumption from appearance or from their country of origin produces errors in both directions.
