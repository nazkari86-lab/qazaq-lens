## The answer in 20 seconds

Kazakh and Russian are not dialects of one language. Kazakh is a Kipchak Turkic language; Russian belongs to the Indo-European family.<sup><a href="#claim-C1">C1</a></sup>

## How different are they?

The distance between Kazakh and Russian is roughly comparable to the distance between Finnish and French — both are spoken in Europe, but they are unrelated. A speaker of one cannot understand the other without learning it.

**Kazakh** belongs to the Kipchak branch of the Turkic language family. It shares structural features with Kyrgyz, Nogai and Karakalpak. Key characteristics include:
- Vowel harmony (vowels within a word harmonise by frontness/backness)
- Agglutinative morphology (meaning is built by adding suffixes to a root)
- Subject-Object-Verb word order
- No grammatical gender
- Core vocabulary with Turkic, Persian and Arabic roots

**Russian** is an East Slavic language in the Indo-European family, related to Ukrainian and Belarusian. It has:
- No vowel harmony
- Inflectional morphology with grammatical gender (masculine, feminine, neuter)
- Flexible but often Subject-Verb-Object word order in neutral sentences
- Core vocabulary with Slavic and Church Slavonic roots<sup><a href="#claim-C1">C1</a></sup>

## Why are both heard in Kazakhstan?

Kazakhstan has a complex multilingual history. Russian spread across the region during the Imperial Russian period (19th–early 20th century) and then more intensively during the Soviet era through settlement, education and industrialisation. Russian became the dominant language of administration, cities and higher education. Many Kazakhs — particularly urban and northern populations — grew up using Russian as a first language or primary working language.

Since independence in 1991, Kazakh has been promoted as the state language. Efforts to expand Kazakh-language education, media and official communication have produced generational differences: younger urban Kazakhstanis often have stronger Kazakh than their parents' generation.

The legal roles are distinct: Kazakh is the state language; Russian has an officially defined role in public institutions.<sup><a href="#claim-C2">C2</a></sup>

## The alphabet question

Kazakh has been written in several scripts over its history: Arabic-derived script (before Soviet era), then Latin (1929–1940), then Cyrillic (1940–present). Kazakhstan has been working on a transition back to Latin script, with a revised Kazakh Latin alphabet in development.

Russian uses its own Cyrillic alphabet, which is distinct from the Kazakh Cyrillic script — the two have different letters for sounds unique to each language.

Languages can share a writing system and remain unrelated. English and Vietnamese both use Latin-based alphabets. The Cyrillic connection is a historical artefact of Soviet policy, not evidence of linguistic kinship.<sup><a href="#claim-C3">C3</a></sup>

## Bilingualism in practice

Many Kazakhstanis are bilingual or multilingual. Code-switching between Kazakh and Russian in a single conversation is common in cities. The term "Shala-Kazakh" is sometimes used — with varying registers of respect or criticism — for Kazakhstanis who are more comfortable in Russian than Kazakh. This reflects a real sociolinguistic pattern, not evidence that the languages are the same.

Assuming a Kazakhstani's preferred language from their appearance, ethnicity or city of origin is likely to be wrong in either direction.

## Better phrasing

Say **"Kazakhstan is multilingual"** rather than "everyone speaks Russian" or "Kazakh is Russian." Avoid assuming a person's preferred language from their ethnicity, city or appearance.
