## The answer in 20 seconds

Kazakhstan is not a nuclear-armed state. It inherited the world's fourth-largest nuclear arsenal when the Soviet Union collapsed in 1991, and then voluntarily gave up every weapon — the only country in history to have done so at that scale.<sup><a href="#claim-C2">C2</a></sup>

## The inheritance

When the Soviet Union dissolved in December 1991, its nuclear weapons did not disappear. They were distributed across the territory of four newly independent states: Russia, Ukraine, Belarus and Kazakhstan. Kazakhstan's inheritance was approximately 1,400 strategic nuclear warheads along with delivery infrastructure — placing it, at the moment of independence, ahead of the United Kingdom, France and China in nuclear arsenal size.<sup><a href="#claim-C1">C1</a></sup>

Kazakhstan had not chosen to develop these weapons and had no operational control over them — the warheads remained under Russian command authority. But their physical presence on Kazakhstani territory made the country a nuclear issue of the first order for international security.

## The decision to disarm

Kazakhstan's first president, Nursultan Nazarbayev, had been a consistent advocate for nuclear disarmament — partly shaped by the country's own experience with nuclear testing (see below). The government decided to transfer all nuclear weapons to Russia and accede to the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty as a non-nuclear weapons state.

Under the Lisbon Protocol to the START treaty (1992), Kazakhstan, Ukraine and Belarus agreed to transfer their nuclear weapons to Russia. Kazakhstan completed the transfer process and joined the NPT in 1994, with the last nuclear warheads leaving Kazakhstani territory in 1995.<sup><a href="#claim-C2">C2</a></sup>

This was not an automatic or pressured handover — it involved active negotiations, security guarantees from the United States, Russia and the United Kingdom (the Budapest Memorandum, to which Kazakhstan was a signatory alongside Ukraine and Belarus), and deliberate political choices by the Kazakhstani government.

## The Semipalatinsk Test Site

Before Kazakhstan inherited any weapons, the region already bore the consequences of Soviet nuclear policy. The Semipalatinsk Test Site — known internationally as "The Polygon" — is located in north-east Kazakhstan, in the steppe region around what is now the city of Semey (formerly Semipalatinsk).

Between 1949 and 1989, the Soviet Union conducted **456 nuclear tests** at Semipalatinsk — both atmospheric and underground. The first Soviet nuclear bomb was detonated there in August 1949, less than a month after the United States had conducted its most recent test.<sup><a href="#claim-C3">C3</a></sup>

The site was chosen for its remoteness from Moscow and major Russian cities. It was not remote from Kazakhstani communities. Dozens of settlements were in the fallout radius of atmospheric tests, and the affected area and population were not adequately informed or evacuated during the testing period.

## Health consequences

Research published in peer-reviewed medical literature documents significantly elevated rates of cancer, thyroid disease, cardiovascular disease, immune disorders and reproductive health problems in populations in the Semipalatinsk region. An estimated 1.5 million people were exposed to ionising radiation from the tests.<sup><a href="#claim-C4">C4</a></sup>

The effects are intergenerational — epidemiological studies have found elevated rates of certain conditions in the children and grandchildren of directly exposed populations. The Semipalatinsk region remains a subject of ongoing public health research.

Kazakhstan closed the Semipalatinsk Test Site in 1991 — one of Nazarbayev's first acts as head of state of independent Kazakhstan. The Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty Organization has worked with Kazakhstan on monitoring and remediation at the site.

## The international dimension

Kazakhstan's voluntary denuclearisation is frequently cited in international arms control discussions as a model — a country that had the weapons, chose not to keep them, and has since been a consistent advocate for nuclear disarmament in international forums. Kazakhstan has participated in the Astana process and various multilateral non-proliferation initiatives.

The Budapest Memorandum, which provided security assurances to Kazakhstan, Ukraine and Belarus in exchange for their nuclear weapons, has attracted renewed international attention following Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine. Kazakhstan has not publicly characterised the memorandum in the same terms as Ukraine's government, but the topic is a live one in Kazakhstani foreign policy discussions.

## Accurate phrasing

- **Current status**: Kazakhstan is a non-nuclear weapons state and an NPT member.
- **Historical fact**: Kazakhstan inherited a nuclear arsenal in 1991 and voluntarily gave it up by 1995.
- **Testing legacy**: The Semipalatinsk Test Site operated under Soviet authority and affected Kazakhstani populations — this is not the same as Kazakhstan being a nuclear power.
